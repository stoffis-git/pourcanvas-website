# The Complete "I'm Dead" Folder: Everything Your Family Needs to Find, Access, and Handle Everything After You're Gone

---

You're building this because someone you loved died recently, and the experience showed you exactly how unprepared most families are. That clarity — the kind that only comes from watching someone you care about navigate the worst days of their life — is the right motivation for this project. Use it now, while it's fresh.

This guide will walk you through building a complete "death folder": the document your spouse, children, or whoever handles your affairs can open on day one and actually use. Not an abstract estate plan. Not a will that sits in probate for six months. A practical operational manual for your life, handed off at the moment you're no longer there to explain it.

It will take several hours spread across a few sessions to do this properly. It is worth every minute.

---

## Part 1: Why This Matters More Than a Will

Most people think their estate plan is their will. It's not. Your will is one document in a larger system, and by itself, it cannot help your family on the day you die. Here's why.

### What Happens in the First 72 Hours

When someone dies, the world does not pause for grief. Within 72 hours of a death, a family typically needs to:

- Notify a funeral home and make immediate decisions about the body (burial vs. cremation, viewing, timeline)
- Obtain death certificates — typically 10–15 certified copies, each costing $10–25 depending on your state
- Notify immediate family and close friends
- Contact the deceased's employer (if applicable) to begin HR processes
- Locate insurance policies and initiate claims
- Determine whether there is enough accessible cash to cover immediate expenses (the funeral alone typically runs $8,000–$12,000 for a traditional burial, or $2,000–$5,000 for direct cremation)
- Figure out what bills are due and whether accounts are accessible

That last point is where most families hit a wall: the deceased's bank accounts are frozen as soon as the bank learns of the death. Not eventually. Immediately upon notification. Any account in the deceased's name alone — checking, savings, brokerage — becomes inaccessible until probate establishes who inherits it. That process takes anywhere from four months to over a year depending on your state and the complexity of your estate.

If your family doesn't know about a joint account, or doesn't know your life insurance policy number, or doesn't know where you kept your passwords — those first 72 hours become an emergency on top of a tragedy.

### What a Will Actually Does

A will tells a probate court who gets your stuff. It does not help your family access money. It does not tell them your alarm code. It does not tell them where the life insurance policy is. It does not tell them who your employer is or that you have a 401(k). All of that information lives in your head, and when you're gone, it's gone with you.

The death folder is not a legal document. It is an operational handoff. It answers the question your family will desperately be asking: "What do we do right now, and where do we find everything we need?"

### What Changes When You Have a Complete Folder

With a well-organized folder, your family can:

- Make funeral decisions that match your actual wishes, not their guesses
- Access a joint account or POD account immediately to cover expenses
- Find every insurance policy and begin claims the same week
- Know exactly which subscriptions to cancel and which to keep temporarily
- Access your email and accounts without calling tech support at three companies
- Know who your accountant, lawyer, and financial advisor are and call them directly
- Feel, in the middle of the worst week of their lives, that you thought of them

That last item is not a small thing.

## Part 2: The Minimum Viable Version

If you have two hours and want to cover the most critical 20%, do these five things in this order:

**1. The emergency access document (30 minutes)**
One page: your phone PIN, computer login password, your password manager login (or master password list), and the location of your physical folder. Give this page to your spouse now, sealed in an envelope if you prefer.

**2. Financial accounts summary (45 minutes)**
List every account: bank accounts, retirement accounts, brokerage, life insurance, 401(k). For each: institution name, account number, approximate balance, and how to contact them. Nothing else — just the list.

**3. Location of critical documents (15 minutes)**
Where is your will? Marriage certificate? Car titles? House deed? Social Security cards? Passports? List the physical location of each.

**4. One joint account with enough liquidity (15 minutes)**
If you don't already have a joint checking account with your spouse, open one. Move $5,000–$10,000 into it. This is the account your family uses to pay the funeral home while everything else is frozen.

**5. Beneficiary designations (30 minutes)**
Log into every retirement account and life insurance policy you own. Verify the beneficiary is correct and current. This is the single highest-leverage thing you can do for your family's financial security. Retirement accounts and life insurance with named beneficiaries bypass probate entirely and pay directly to your named person within weeks.

Do the full version. But if you stop here, you've done more than most people ever do.

## Part 3: The Letter to Your Family

This goes at the front of the folder. Not legal, not financial — the human letter. Write it now, while you're thinking about it.

Below is a template with prompts. Fill in what applies. Delete what doesn't.

---

**Template: Letter to My Family**

*Date written: ___________*

To [spouse/partner name], [children's names], and anyone else reading this:

If you're reading this, I'm gone. I hope I had time to say the things I needed to say in person. Whether I did or didn't, I want you to know: [write something true about how you feel about your family — keep it short, keep it honest].

I built this folder because I love you and I didn't want the practical chaos of my death to make the hard days harder. Everything you need to know about my accounts, my wishes, and my life is in here. Please read through it together.

**About the next few days:**
The most urgent things are in Section 1 (First Week Checklist). If you're overwhelmed, start there.

**About money immediately:**
We have a joint checking account at [Bank name]. The account number is in Section 20. There is approximately $______ in it. Use this for immediate expenses including the funeral, if needed. My life insurance policy (Section 18) should pay out within 30–60 days of a claim being filed — see that section for the policy numbers and how to file.

**About my wishes:**
My funeral and burial wishes are written out in Section 14–15. Please honor them if you can. If circumstances make it impossible, do what you need to do. I'll understand.

**About the house:**
Section [X] has the operations manual for the house — the alarm code, the service providers I trusted, the maintenance schedule. Don't skip this section. There's stuff in there no one else knows.

**About the passwords:**
My password manager is [name]. The master password or emergency access instructions are in Section 2. Do not skip this — almost everything is in there.

**What I want for you:**
[Write something true here. What do you want for your kids in the years after you're gone? What do you want your spouse to know about grief and moving forward? Don't overthink it. Write what you'd want to say.]

I love you. I'm sorry you're reading this. Now go take care of each other, and use this folder.

[Your name]
[Date]

---

*Update this letter every year. Date every version. Keep the last three versions.*

### Section 1: First-Week Checklist for Survivors

Before reading the rest of this folder, use this checklist.

**Day 1**
- [ ] Contact a funeral home (see Section 14 for my specific wishes and pre-planning details)
- [ ] Notify immediate family and close friends
- [ ] Do NOT notify banks yet — wait until you have death certificates in hand
- [ ] Locate my phone and computer — PINs and passwords are in Section 2
- [ ] Find the emergency cash account (joint checking at [Bank], Section 20)
- [ ] Call my employer's HR department: [Employer name, HR number — see Section 8]

**Days 2–3**
- [ ] Order 12–15 certified death certificates from the funeral home (they handle this). Cost: approximately $10–25 each depending on your state. Order more than you think you need — you'll send them to every financial institution, insurance company, and government agency.
- [ ] Locate my will (see Section 17 for location) and contact my attorney: [Attorney name and number — see Section 17]
- [ ] Contact my life insurance company to begin claim: [Policy numbers — see Section 18]
- [ ] If I had a financial advisor, call them: [Name and number — see Section 19]

**Days 4–7**
- [ ] Notify Social Security Administration: 1-800-772-1213. If I was receiving benefits, they must be notified immediately and may need to return the payment for the month of death.
- [ ] Contact my bank(s) with a death certificate to begin the estate process (do NOT do this until you have certificates — you can't undo notification)
- [ ] Begin canceling or transferring subscriptions (see Section 9)
- [ ] Notify relevant government agencies if I received Medicare, Medicaid, or veterans benefits

**First month**
- [ ] File for life insurance benefits (most pay within 30–60 days of receiving a completed claim and death certificate)
- [ ] Consult an estate attorney or my existing attorney about probate
- [ ] Contact my accountant about any tax filings required: [Name and number — see Section 27]
- [ ] Review and update beneficiaries on accounts that transfer to you

---

**Common mistake:** People notify banks immediately, before they have death certificates. The bank then freezes all solo accounts and the family has no access to anything while waiting for certificates. Wait until you have certificates in hand. Keep the joint account open and funded for this reason.

**Medical directives (keep originals in this section):**

An advance directive (also called a living will) tells medical providers what interventions you want or don't want if you're incapacitated and can't speak for yourself. It addresses: ventilator use, artificial nutrition, resuscitation (DNR), organ donation.

A healthcare proxy (also called a healthcare power of attorney or medical POA) designates a specific person to make medical decisions on your behalf.

Both documents should be:
- Filed with your primary care physician
- Stored in this binder
- Held by whoever you've designated as your healthcare proxy

**How to get these documents:**

- Attorney-prepared: $200–500, worth it if your wishes are complex or your family is complicated.

### Section 12: Durable Power of Attorney for Finances

**Update frequency:** When your designated agent changes. Review every 3–5 years.

This document matters for the period before death, not after. If you become ill or incapacitated without one, a court may need to appoint a conservator — an expensive, slow process.

### Section 16: Obituary

**Update frequency:** Draft it now. Update as your life changes.

Writing a draft obituary now saves your family from writing one under grief in 24 hours. It also ensures the facts are right.

Include:
- Full name and nicknames
- Date and place of birth
- City of residence at time of death
- Surviving family members
- Career summary (one or two sentences)
- Notable accomplishments or community involvement
- Hobbies and interests your family should mention
- Any specific newspapers or publications you'd like it submitted to
- Online memorial platform preferences (e.g., Legacy.com)

**Draft obituary template:**

[Name] of [city], [state], passed away on [date]. [He/She/They] was [age].

[Name] was born on [date] in [city, state] to [parents' names]. [He/She/They] [brief life story — 2–3 sentences: education, career, major life events].

[Name] is survived by [list surviving family members].

[Optional: paragraph about personality, passions, accomplishments].

In lieu of flowers, the family asks for donations to [charity, if applicable].

A service will be held [when/where, or "at a later date"].

**The account freeze problem:** Every account solely in your name is frozen immediately when the bank learns of your death. Joint accounts remain accessible to the surviving joint holder. Payable-on-death accounts transfer directly to the named beneficiary without probate — usually within 1–2 weeks of presenting a death certificate.

**Action required now:** Set up POD designations on every bank account you own. This is free and takes 10 minutes at your bank or through online banking. It may be the single most practical thing you can do for your family.

## Part 5: The Account Freeze Problem — Full Explanation

When a bank learns a sole account holder has died, they freeze the account. This is automatic and immediate. Your family cannot withdraw funds, cannot pay bills from the account, cannot access anything.

This is not a bug — it's a legal protection for the estate. But it creates a real problem on days 1–7 when expenses are piling up and probate hasn't started.

**What stays accessible:**
- Joint accounts — the surviving joint owner retains full access
- POD (Payable on Death) accounts — accessible to the named beneficiary within 1–2 weeks of presenting a death certificate
- Life insurance proceeds — paid directly to named beneficiaries, usually within 30–60 days
- Retirement accounts (IRAs, 401k) — paid to named beneficiaries, typically within 2–4 weeks

**What gets frozen:**
- Any bank account, brokerage account, or savings account in the deceased's name alone

**How to protect your family now:**

1. Open a joint checking account with your spouse. Fund it with $10,000–$20,000 minimum. This is the operating account for the estate.

2. Add POD designations to every bank account you own. This is free, takes 10 minutes at your bank, and allows named beneficiaries to access funds within days — not months.

3. Add TOD (Transfer on Death) to brokerage accounts. Same mechanism, same benefit.

4. Name beneficiaries on every retirement account. This is the single most important beneficiary designation because retirement accounts are often the largest assets people own. Without a beneficiary, the account goes through probate, which delays access and can trigger unfavorable tax treatment.

5. At least one account should be joint. Ideally: the account your family pays bills from. If every account is sole, your family is locked out of everything until probate establishes authority — which can take months.

## Part 6: Medical and End-of-Life Documents — Full Guide

You need three things:

**1. Advance Directive (Living Will)**

Tells medical providers what to do if you can't speak for yourself. Covers:
- CPR/resuscitation
- Mechanical ventilation
- Artificial nutrition and hydration
- Sedation
- Organ donation

Without this document, medical providers default to doing everything to keep you alive, regardless of what you would have wanted. With it, they follow your wishes.

**How to get one:**
- Free: Five Wishes (fivewishes.org) — legally valid in most states, conversational format
- Free: Your state's department of health provides state-specific forms
- $15–50: Notarized at a UPS Store, bank, or online (Notarize.com)
- $200–500: Attorney-prepared, recommended if your situation is complex

**2. Healthcare Proxy (Medical POA)**

Names a specific person to make medical decisions if you're incapacitated. This is different from the advance directive — it's who decides, not just what you've written down. Your proxy handles situations the directive didn't anticipate.

Choose someone who:
- Will actually honor your wishes, not their own instincts
- Can handle pressure from other family members
- Is geographically accessible enough to show up

**3. POLST (Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment)**

Different from an advance directive — this is a doctor's order, not a patient's preference document. More specific about immediate medical interventions. Relevant if you have a terminal illness or serious condition. Your doctor prepares this with you.

**Storage:**
- Original advance directive: in this binder, Section 11
- Copy: with your primary care physician (ask them to add to your medical record)
- Copy: with your designated healthcare proxy
- Copy: in your car's glove compartment, or a wallet card indicating where the original is

## Part 7: Pre-Planning Your Funeral

The average family spends $8,000–$12,000 on a traditional funeral — often within 24 hours of a death, under intense emotional stress, with a funeral home salesperson presenting options. This is not a good situation.

Pre-planning does not mean pre-paying (though pre-paying is an option). It means making the decisions now so your family doesn't have to.

**Decisions to make now:**

Burial vs. cremation: A personal, sometimes religious decision. Note your choice clearly. If you have strong feelings, explain them briefly.

Viewing/visitation: Do you want one? Open casket, closed, or neither?

Service: Religious or secular? If religious, what denomination, what traditions? Specific readings, music, or prayers?

Location: Church, funeral home, graveside, or a meaningful non-traditional location?

Guests: Private (family only) or open to the community?

Flowers vs. donations: If you prefer donations in your memory, name the specific charity.

Obituary: Who submits it? To which publications? (A draft is in Section 16.)

Reception: Do you want one? Where? Any specific food or drink that would feel like you?

**Cost reality check for your family:**

Write an approximate budget or at least a preference range. Your family should not feel obligated to spend more than you would have wanted. If simplicity mattered to you, say so explicitly.

**Pre-paying:**

If you pre-pay, the price is locked in at today's rates. The contract is transferable if you move. Make sure to document:
- Funeral home name and contact
- Contract number
- What is covered and what is not
- Whether the contract is transferable and portable

Pre-paid funeral costs: $5,000–$15,000 depending on services selected. Some states have a state guarantee fund that protects pre-paid funds if the funeral home goes out of business — check your state's funeral regulatory board.

## Part 8: Special Situations

### Small Business Owners

If you own a business, your death creates an immediate operational crisis alongside the personal one. Your folder must include:

- Business structure (LLC, S-corp, sole proprietorship, partnership)
- Operating agreement or partnership agreement location
- What happens to the business under the agreement at death
- Key employees and who runs day-to-day operations
- Business bank accounts (separate from personal)
- Business loans and lines of credit
- Business insurance (key person insurance, business interruption)
- Client list and status of major contracts
- CPA and business attorney contacts
- Whether the business has a buy-sell agreement and who the purchaser is
- Business valuation (updated within last 2 years)

**The succession question:** What do you want to happen to the business? Sold? Transferred to a family member? Wound down? If you haven't answered this legally (buy-sell agreement, succession plan), it defaults to whatever your estate plan says — which may mean your spouse inherits a business they have no ability to run.

### Blended Families

Blended families — with children from prior relationships, stepchildren, or ex-spouses — are the most likely to have estate disputes. The death folder doesn't resolve legal disputes, but it documents your intentions clearly.

Additional documentation for blended families:
- Explicit note in the letter about your intentions for all children
- Beneficiary designations checked and updated (your children from a prior relationship may or may not be named — be intentional)
- Any divorce decrees that affect your estate (required support payments, restrictions on remarriage clauses)
- Any pre-nuptial or post-nuptial agreements and their location
- Specific property that is intended for specific children

**The beneficiary trap:** Life insurance and retirement accounts pass by beneficiary designation, not by will. If you meant to name your current spouse but still have an ex-spouse listed from 15 years ago, the ex gets the money. Regardless of your will. Regardless of your divorce decree (with some state-specific exceptions). Check every beneficiary designation right now.

### Adults Caring for Aging Parents

If you are simultaneously managing your own affairs and those of an elderly parent, you need a section for each.

For your parent's affairs:
- Whether you hold their POA, healthcare proxy, or are their guardian/conservator
- Their financial accounts and where they're held
- Their insurance policies
- Their healthcare providers and living situation
- Their wishes and advance directives
- What happens to their care if you die first

This is an often-overlooked scenario. If you are your parent's caregiver or the person managing their finances, and you die unexpectedly, they may have no one to manage their care. Document this explicitly and identify a backup.

### Renters vs. Homeowners

**If you rent:**

Section 24 (Real Estate) is simpler, but you still need to document:
- Landlord name and contact
- Lease terms and expiration
- Security deposit amount and where it was paid
- How to terminate the lease (some states allow lease termination at death with notice; your family should not continue paying rent on an empty apartment)
- Renters insurance policy and how to claim

**If you own:**

All of Section 24 plus: the mortgage payoff process, whether the mortgage has a credit life insurance policy (some do — it pays off the mortgage at death), home equity lines of credit, and any liens on the property.

## Part 10: The Maintenance System

A death folder that isn't maintained is worse than no folder — it gives false confidence. Here's the system:

### What Triggers an Immediate Update

- New account opened or closed
- Password changed
- New property purchased or sold
- New life insurance policy
- Beneficiary changed on any account
- New vehicle
- Move to a new home
- Marriage, divorce, or new child
- Change in employment with benefits implications
- New business interest

**The rule:** When the event happens, update the folder within 30 days.

### The Twice-Yearly Review

Set two calendar reminders, 6 months apart. Use these dates consistently — many people use daylight saving time changes as a trigger. During the review:

- [ ] Update account balances (approximate)
- [ ] Verify all passwords are current in the password manager
- [ ] Check beneficiary designations on all accounts (log in and verify)
- [ ] Update the subscriptions list
- [ ] Check document expiration dates (passport, driver's license)
- [ ] Review the house operations manual for anything that's changed
- [ ] Date the review on the cover page of the binder
- [ ] Tell whoever knows about the folder that it's been updated

### Dating Every Page

Every page you print should be dated. Use the footer in your word processor or spreadsheet software. When your family reads this folder, they need to know whether the phone number for your plumber is from last month or from 2019. A date on the page solves this.

### Version Control

Keep the last two versions of updated pages. When you update a section, date the new page, print it, and replace the old one — but keep the old one in a "previous versions" pocket at the back of the binder for one year before discarding.

## Part 11: How to Tell Your Family

Building the folder matters. But it only works if your family knows it exists and can find it.

### The Conversation

Have it directly. You don't need to make it dramatic — it's a practical conversation about practical preparedness.

Something like: "I've put together a folder with everything you'd need if something happened to me. I want to show you where it is and walk you through the main sections. It'll take 20 minutes. I'd feel a lot better knowing you knew where this was."

Walk them through:
- Physical location of the binder
- The section structure
- Where passwords and digital access are
- That it should be updated and how to know when it was last updated
- Who else knows about it

**Who else should know:**
- Your executor (they need to know this exists and where it is)
- Your adult children (if applicable)
- Possibly your closest trusted friend, in case your family is incapacitated

**What not to do:** Don't simply put the folder somewhere and assume they'll find it. The folder is useless if no one knows it exists.

### Physical Storage

**A fireproof, waterproof home safe is the correct answer.**

Recommended type: A small fireproof and waterproof document safe. Look for UL-rated fire resistance of at least 1 hour at 1,700°F and water resistance.

Specific models worth considering:
- SentrySafe SFW123GDC (fireproof, waterproof, digital lock): approximately $100–130
- SentrySafe 1200 (fireproof, key lock, no electronic components): approximately $35–50 (note: not waterproof)
- Honeywell 1104 (fireproof, waterproof, combination lock): approximately $70–90

If you use a biometric or electronic lock: tape the override key or backup code to the inside lid or store it in a second location. A dead battery should not make your death folder inaccessible.

**What goes in the safe:**
- Original will
- Original advance directive
- Passports
- Social Security cards
- Birth certificates
- Marriage certificate
- The master password page (if you have one)
- The death folder binder itself, or at minimum its password/access information

### Digital Backup Strategy

The physical binder is the primary document. A digital backup is a secondary copy.

**Option 1: Encrypted cloud storage**
Store a PDF copy of the folder in a secure cloud folder (Google Drive, iCloud, Dropbox). Share that folder with your spouse or designated person. Understand this has security implications — use 2FA on the account and don't store seed phrases digitally.

**Option 2: Encrypted USB drive**
Use VeraCrypt or BitLocker to create an encrypted volume on a USB drive. Store the drive in the safe. Give the decryption password to your spouse separately.

**Option 3: Password manager secure notes**
Many password managers (Bitwarden, 1Password) have a secure notes feature. Store key information there — accessible via emergency access after death.

**What should NOT be stored in unencrypted cloud storage:** Seed phrases, full Social Security numbers, account passwords, or anything that creates a security risk if the account is compromised.
